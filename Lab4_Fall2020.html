Lab 4 – Sampling Distributions

Sampling distributions

A sample statistic is simply a function of a random sample. Some examples include the sample mean (sum the sample values and divide by the sample size) and sample median (order the samples and find the middle value). The distribution of a statistic is called a sampling distribution. For example, compare the two statements:

  1. “What’s the chance that the lifetime of a lightbulb is greater than 750 hours?”

  2. “What’s the chance that the average lifetime of 100 lightbulbs is greater than 750 hours?”

These two statements are similar (‘the chance some number is greater than 750’), but the top concerns a single observation while the bottom concerns 100 samples. To answer the bottom question, we will need to know the distribution of the sample mean.

The key is to realize that both questions deal with the same population (both the single lightbulb and 100 lightbulbs are drawn from the same distribution), and, as it turns out, by studying the sampling distribution we can make inferences about the population as a whole.

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

Question one can often be very challenging to directly answer while question two can be very easy (provided we sample enough lightbulbs). This is due to the Central Limit Theorem (CLT). The CLT basically states that the distribution of the sample mean will be approximately normal for a large enough sample size. In the context above, this means that the distribution of the sample average of 100 (or 100,000) lightbulbs will look normally distributed, even though the distribution of a single lightbulb might be highly skewed and non-normal.

Standard error

The standard error is simply the standard deviation of the sampling distribution.

For example, if we kept taking 100 lightbulbs off of the factory line and computing the average lifetime, we would expect the average lifetime itself to vary between samples of size 100. This variability is due to the randomness of the individual lightbulbs, so the variability of the sample mean and the individual lightbulbs is related, but not exactly the same. To avoid confusion, we will call the standard deviation of the average lifetime the standard error and the standard deviation of the individual lightbulbs…well…the standard deviation.

# Set Seed
set.seed(42)

# Filter to areas of Georgia
election_Georgia = election %>% filter(State == 'Georgia')
# Plot histogram to view distribution of Democratic votes
election_Georgia %>% ggplot(aes(x=DemVotes)) + geom_histogram(bins=40)

Do you think DemVotes is normally distributed? The ‘helper’ function coded below takes the arguments dataset, size, variable and outputs a dataframe of 5000 simulated sample means. Try looking at the head() of the sample_size_10 dataframe to get a feel for the sample_variable() function.

Take a look at the density plot below. What happens as the sample size, \(n\), increases?

# Function to simulate sample means for given dataset,size,variable
sample_variable = function(dataset,size,variable) {
  rep_sample_n(dataset, size = size, replace = T, reps = 5000) %>% 
  summarise(sample_mean = mean(!!sym(variable)),.groups = 'drop') %>%
  .[,2] 
}

# Simulates sample means for various sample sizes
sample_size_10 = sample_variable(election_Georgia,10,"DemVotes")
sample_size_30 = sample_variable(election_Georgia,30,"DemVotes")
sample_size_75 = sample_variable(election_Georgia,75,"DemVotes")
sample_size_150 = sample_variable(election_Georgia,150,"DemVotes")


# Creates a density plot for each sample size
data.frame(sample_size_10,sample_size_30,sample_size_75,sample_size_150) %>%
  gather("Size","Value") %>%
  ggplot(aes(x=Value,col=Size)) + geom_density() +
  scale_color_manual(labels = c('n = 10','n = 30','n = 75','n = 150'),
                       values=c("#F1BB7B", "#FD6467", "#5B1A18", "#D67236"))
